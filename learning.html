October 15, 2020: Building blocks
My journey with the Students as Partners Program (SaPP) began in the early days of September when I was desperately searching for work.

As an international student, my university fees had just been hiked. My father had taken a pay cut and I had earned a small stipend in Indian rupees, which amounted to barely CAD300, working as a business media intern. In my first year, I had spent a lot of time getting to know my professors and building relationships with them. That bore fruit this year, when I reached out to all of them to ask if they needed a Student Partner for Winter 2021. My Environmental Studies professor, David Hugill, flooded his department's mailing list with an email asking other faculty members if they had any available opportunities. And then I met Professor Dipto Sarkar, now my faculty partner for this project.

Both of us have an Indian background, so we were able to get along on the cultural front. Otherwise too, it was easy to relate to Dipto and start learning from him. GIS seemed like something that was missing from my life given my love of data. Dipto not only told me about the specific GIS in his course, but also showed me how I could apply it to my program, journalism. I had never given a second thought to the interactive maps in The New York Times' articles, but suddenly it all made sense.

In our first meeting, Dipto and I spoke about what GEOG4001 would entail, what I could assist with and what both of us could learn from each other. We also drafted the application for the program. Our teamwork was proving to be efficient. Later, I looked over the initial setup instructions for the course and built my GitHub front portfolio. Now, I'm developing this blog on Atom, something I wish I'd known about much earlier in my life. The best part about coding is how organized it is, just like mathematics. I'm grateful to have had this opportunity and also to be able to learn so much in such a short period of time. Stay tuned for more updates on this topsy-turvy journey! There is more adventure to come.
November 2, 2020: Mapbox, blogging and a confused student
The biggest irony of being a journalist learning how to code is unlearning the use of punctuation.

Over the course of the past 2 weeks, I have worked with Dipto to tackle the first assignment for his course: an interactive map created with Mapbox. Dipto has been incredibly resourceful and helpful, providing me with tools to learn at every stage where I find myself stuck. I often get stuck when I see unfamiliar language or code, but that is the essence of learning: diving into the unknown without an oxygen tank. So I started learning, and with Dipto's help converting shapefiles into geoJSON's and moving through the assignment instructions, I managed to develop a map using Mapbox.

I used my home state Odisha's geographical data and interesting water table data to create a map. It shows points that are surrounded by abundant water sources but are hotspots of scarcity themselves. I used polygons from a Odisha districts file which Dipto sourced for me, and line and point files which the government of Odisha had available. Find my map here.

The next thing I want to address in this reflection is the amount of time it has taken me to develop these simple things. The webpage for these blog-like updates has taken me until now to develop and add content to, but it is so easy. This is especially useful as I progress to publishing my own blog without using a pre-existing software, so I can customize it the way I like it. In fact, I can add as much punctuation as I want because I won't be writing news on there.

I find it quite amusing that journalists are told to avoid semi-colons like the plague but Cascading Style Sheets or CSS, is practically impossible without their use. Indeed, this is a challenging project which is training me in skills that I will use at the back-end of my career as a journalist, hopefully a multimedia journalist, while avoiding semi-colons in my newswriting. Now, the reason why I mentioned 'confused student' in the title for this segment is because confusion to me is often a catalyst to learn and do more work. It took me some time to organize this webpage into rows of table (can you believe it?) instead of boxes. This was Dipto's pro tip! So my confusion with tr tags and td tags forced me to spend some time on w3schools.com learning how to do basic HTML and CSS. My next challenge which I am not even confused but rather baffled by, is Javascript to make my map even better. Let's see how it goes!
November 23, 2020: One down, two to go, and the challenges of JS
It took a month. Dipto (with his extensive knowledge of coding) and I (with my fledgling knowledge of code) took a month, collectively, to complete Assignment 1. It is through mental challenges and opportunities for learning that the SaPP program becomes especially enriching for students like myself, who are in a field completely different than their own.
My code was working just fine until the first chapter (look at me using technical .js terms!). After days brainstorming and de-bugging, Dipto found a missing comma and an extra bracket that was causing the issues. Once those were gone, the map worked just fine. It could definitely be prettier, but the technicality here is more important than the aesthetics of the map-not that the map looks bad.
After the map was ready, I had to integrate it into my website/front-facing portfolio. In that, I was once again stuck. But based on my learning thus far, I decided to try finding a solution myself rather than email Dipto right away. So I did. After exploring some websites and consulting my brother, I realized the simplest solution was to rename the story.js and its corresponding index.html file such that they could be in the same folder as my website's files. Perhaps there is a better way to organize these files, but that's for another time.

I'm proud of this achievement, but there is a long way to go. There is more to learn and there are two assignments to get through before the SaPP term ends. I am confident that even if Dipto and I are unable to test those assignments in time, we will both have gained value through this term. For Dipto, I believe the value lays in learning which assignments work and which need tweaking. The challenge there is that my level of knowledge in coding is very different from that of his students. My level is lower, and that is probably better for Dipto to make the assignment descriptions, for instance, comprehensive and accessible to all.
December 1, 2020: Assignment 2
My struggle with coding and actually helping Dipto with his assignments is becoming a theme in these journals. Of course, that is a reflection of the truth. But, this entry is more to describe an application of learning from SaPP that I have applied outside school. I recently got hired as a volunteer staff writer at Spheres of Influence, which is an analytical journalism/international policy publication. Until now, they hadn't been using data or geospatial elements to complement their stories. In my interview, I mentioned using simple tools to make their articles more interactive for readers to stay hooked to them. I suggested Mapbox, something so complicated for beginners like myself, but so wonderful for people who are just looking at the visuals and learning from them. Dipto has taught me to make those maps and now I can teach others while also helping enhance a newly-formed not-for-profit.
Besides that, it is time to get started on the second assignment. School has been tectic and I've been finding it challenging to allot regular hours to work. Instead, I've been working 4-5 hours at a stretch once in 3 days. However, those 4-5 hours do not yield as much as they would if I were actually building something rather than fixing what was going wrong. That's a part of being an amateur coder. Dipto reassures me that small steps too are useful to him, and I hope that that is true. We are going to start workign on Assignment 2, which builds on the first assignment but adds the elements of geocoding and automated data integration to the maps. I already know by the sound of it that it will be challenging but we've got this!

December 18, 2020: Wrapping up
The meeting with Dipto went well! We had an honest conversation about what we achieved and what we will do from hereon. The first thing we did was adjust the second assignment to make sure everything worked. The two parts I was missing were:
  1. A single space in an element such that a syntax error made the entire .setLngLat function redundant in the website
  2. The wrong address of the map style. Here, I had chosen to make an entirely new map and manually add points, whereas I should have simplified the code and used only a base map from Mapbox. Dipto sent me the code for this and I learned how to do it.

The assignment works now. I have to add more things to the website to make it a standalone page. After that, I'm going to collate all the different pages to create one front-facing portfolio. The website, at the moment, looks something like the image on the right.
